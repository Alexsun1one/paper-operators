# Story Card Grammar

Use this reference when the asset role is `story/book card`, including novels, biographies, case studies, fables, historical episodes, product origin stories, and book explainers.

A story card is not a mood board. It must help a reader understand the story faster.

## Story Slots

Before prompting, identify these slots:

```text
protagonist:
initial state:
pressure or conflict:
guides / witnesses / opposing forces:
turning point:
choice:
consequence:
transformation:
final image role:
```

Not every slot needs a label, but the scene should contain the ones that make the story legible.

## Common Story Relations

- **Transformation**: a character changes state because of what they see, lose, learn, or choose.
- **Moral choice**: the image must show the fork, not only the good ending.
- **Journey**: stations matter because they change the protagonist, not because they are sequential.
- **Revelation**: a hidden truth is exposed by a letter, light, mirror, archive, ghost, map, or witness.
- **Tragedy**: the consequence is unavoidable after a missed choice; do not draw it as a generic sad scene.
- **Quest**: the path tests values; labels should mark trials, allies, costs, and return.

## Operator Roles For Stories

Pick one paper operator job:

- **Archive Tender**: opens letters, memory trays, evidence drawers, or source pages.
- **Light Catcher**: reveals a moral or emotional truth.
- **Thread Runner**: guides a path through scenes or chapters.
- **Gate Builder**: marks a choice, threshold, or point of no return.
- **Tradeoff Weigher**: stages competing values or costs.
- **Care Folder**: shelters a fragile memory, promise, or relationship.

The operator is usually outside the story world, like a stagehand, reader, archivist, or lantern bearer. Do not turn the operator into the protagonist unless the user asks.

## Composition Patterns

### One-Card Arc

Use for a whole book or familiar story.

- Arrange 3-5 raised stations on one tabletop stage.
- Use one continuous ribbon, light path, or sequence of windows.
- Put the protagonist at the start and the changed state at the end.
- Add a single focal action by the operator: revealing, threading, lighting, weighing, or opening.

### Split Moral Choice

Use when the story turns on a decision.

- Put the choice in the center.
- Put pressure/cost on one side and transformation/consequence on the other.
- The operator physically opens, blocks, weighs, or lights the choice.

### Evidence Archive

Use for biographies, historical cases, investigations, and non-fiction books.

- Use trays, source cards, letters, maps, artifacts, and a final claim.
- The operator sorts evidence into a readable path.
- Use labels for evidence roles, not long citations.

## Label Rules

Use 4-8 short labels for complex story cards.

Good labels:

- `CALL`
- `CHOICE`
- `COST`
- `WITNESS`
- `RETURN`
- `CHANGE`
- `PROOF`
- `PROMISE`

Avoid:

- plot paragraphs
- character quotes unless the exact wording is the point
- labels that reveal no relation, such as `SCENE 1`, `SCENE 2`, `SCENE 3`

## QA

Accept if:

- the protagonist or central subject is obvious
- the turning point is visible
- the final state differs from the initial state
- labels help with story structure rather than summarizing every event
- the card can be understood by someone who knows the story only vaguely

Regenerate if:

- it is only atmosphere
- it is only a timeline
- the ending appears without the choice or cost
- the operator merely points at the story
- the image could be used for any classic novel by changing labels
